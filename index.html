Thirty-three thousand people answered that question on their phones, three times a day, for weeks. Researchers used the 1.7 million answers to settle an old fight: does more money keep making us happier, or does it stop mattering?

The conflict

Two famous studies. Opposite answers.

Both were published in the same journal. Both used real data from thousands of Americans. They could not both be right.

Kahneman & Deaton2010

Happiness climbs with income, then flattens around $75,000. Past that, more money buys no more day-to-day well-being.

Killingsworth2021

Happiness keeps rising with income — steadily, well past $200,000. No plateau in sight.

The "$75,000 plateau" became one of the most-quoted facts in psychology. The second study seemed to demolish it. So in 2022 the two camps did something unusual: they teamed up to find out who was right.

The reanalysis

An argument, run as an experiment.

Daniel Kahneman (the "plateau" author) and Matthew Killingsworth (the "keeps rising" author) ran a joint re-analysis, with a third researcher, Barbara Mellers, as a neutral referee. They call it adversarial collaboration: disagree first, then go find the answer together.

Each buzz asked the same thing you answered at the top — how do you feel right now? — on a smooth slider, not a yes/no box. That detail turns out to matter enormously.

The resolution

It depends who you already are.

When they split people by how happy they tend to be, the single curve broke into a family of curves. Drag the dial below from often down to usually good, and watch how the very same dollars do completely different things.

For the happiest people, money keeps working — and even speeds up past $100,000. For people in the middle, it keeps helping, gently. Only at the bottom does the famous plateau appear. Averaging everyone together hid all three patterns inside one tidy line.

The catch

Where money quietly stops.

For the least-happy fifth of people, more income relieves real distress — but only up to about $100,000. Beyond that, the line goes flat. Some kinds of unhappiness don't have a price: heartbreak, bereavement, clinical depression.

That's also the key to the original mistake. The 2010 study asked yes/no mood questions that almost everyone answered "yes" to — so it could only really detect unhappiness. And unhappiness does plateau. Flip the label, and the old "$75k" finding turns out to be right — just about a different thing.

The reality check

Real — but smaller than you'd think.

Money genuinely moves mood — but only a little. The correlation between income and day-to-day happiness is just 0.09, and across the study's whole range, from $15,000 to $250,000 a year, typical happiness differs by about five points on a 100-point scale.

Here's the size of that in everyday terms. Picture a big raise — quadrupling your income — and ask how much it changes how good an average day feels. Now compare it to other ordinary things. The longer the bar, the more that thing shifts your mood, up or down.

The raise (in rose) barely registers next to a headache, and lands about where an ordinary weekend does. Income matters — it's just a small ingredient of a good day, not the recipe.

How they settled it

Two rivals, one honest answer.

The quiet hero of this paper isn't a number — it's the method. Two scientists who'd staked their names on opposite claims sat down with each other's data, agreed in advance what would count as evidence, and let the analysis decide. Both turned out to be partly right. One publicly revised a famous conclusion.

"We benefited from luck… the close correspondence we found is a better result than social scientists can reasonably expect, even when their hypotheses are correct." — Killingsworth, Kahneman & Mellers, 2023
